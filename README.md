## Chapter Summary

### Chapter 1 — Rings and Ideals

This chapter provides a broad overview of commutative algebra and introduces many of the themes developed later in the book.

The exercises introduce the spectrum of a ring, which becomes a central object in more advanced studies.

### Chapter 2 — Modules

One of the most important results in the chapter is Nakayama's Lemma (Proposition 2.6), which appears throughout the book.

The exercises also introduce direct limits and absolutely flat rings.

### Chapter 3 — Rings and Modules of Fractions

The notion of $S(\mathfrak{a})$ is particularly important for primary decomposition.

Exercise 3.8 shows that saturated multiplicatively closed subsets of $A$ are in one-to-one correspondence with distinct localizations $S^{-1}A$.

### Chapter 4 — Primary Decomposition

The results in Proposition 4.4 and 4.8 are fundamental for understanding primary ideal structure.

Proposition 4.9 is useful for computations.

Exercise 4.13 (iv) has typo issue. It should be: $\mathfrak{p}^{(n)}=\mathfrak{p}^{n} \Leftrightarrow \mathfrak{p}^{n}$ is $\mathfrak{p}$-primary.

### Chapter 5 — Integral Dependence and Valuations

The most important chapter in the book.

**Important results:** Lying Over Theorem (Theorem 5.10), Noether Normalization Lemma (Exercise 5.16).

Exercise 5.10 connects the going-up and going-down properties of a ring homomorphism with topological properties of the induced map on spectra, but the ideas are not used further in the book.

Exercise 5.25 uses Jacobson rings to generalize Zariski's Lemma.

### Chapter 6 & 7 — Chain Conditions & Noetherian Rings

Most exercises further refine old concepts rather than introducing new ideas.

### Chapter 8 — Artinian Rings

An important result is Theorem 8.5.

Exercise 8.3 provides another generalization of Zariski's Lemma.

### Chapter 9 — Discrete Valuation Rings and Dedekind Domains

This chapter is closely connected to algebraic number theory. Propositions 9.2 and 9.3 appear repeatedly throughout the exercises.

### Chapter 10 — Completions

Introduces completions and topological rings. For Noetherian rings, Proposition 10.13 is important. Proposition 10.15 and Krull intersection theorem (Proposition 10.17) are useful in computations.

The exercises show that two linear topologies defined by two filtrations are equivalent when each filtration eventually dominates the other. 
 
The proof of Hensel's lemma (Exercise 10.9) is straightforward, but it involves many details that must be handled carefully.

### Chapter 11 — Dimension Theory

The central result of the chapter is the Dimension Theorem for Noetherian local rings (Theorem 11.14).

This chapter mainly serves as an introduction to the subject, and a deeper understanding generally requires further study.
